# PLAN: What Do We Mean by *Language*?

**Last updated:** 2026-04-26
**Primary target:** *Language Sciences*
**Status:** Planned, not drafted
**Working title:** "What Do We Mean by *Language*? A Pluralist Map for the *Language Sciences*"
**Word budget:** 8000-10000 words.

## Thesis

The label *language* covers a small number of recurrent sense-clusters. Each cluster earns realist scientific standing only where it is stabilised and projectible for a field purpose. Confusions arise when the shared label is mistaken for a shared kind, producing slippage between clusters. HPC theory adds the discipline that lexical pluralism does not: each cluster must pass projectibility and homeostasis tests, and slippage between clusters is a diagnosable failure mode.

## Why this paper, given Santana 2016

Santana 2016 ("What Is Language?", *Ergo*) is the closest published precedent. He argues for pluralism on three ontologies (psychological, social, abstract), each satisfying three criteria (existence, primacy in linguistic practice, reasonable use of the term). He layers two strictures: pluralism must not collapse into isolationism between subdisciplines; pluralism forbids agnosticism about which ontology a given inquiry commits to.

Structural overlap with this paper is large. Honest engagement is required. The paper makes four moves Santana doesn't:

1. **A more granular cluster taxonomy.** Santana's three ontologies cover the right ground for a philosophical paper, but they merge what working scientists treat as distinct: developmental capacities, online processing capacities, and cross-species comparative capacities all collapse into "psychological"; named-language objects, registers, and identity-marking practices collapse into "social"; abstract formal systems, methodological constructs, and analytical posits collapse into "abstract." A working scientist asks for finer cuts.
2. **HPC discipline.** Santana's criteria (existence, primacy in practice, reasonable use of the term) are permissive — they admit any cluster that some linguists work on. HPC adds projectibility (the cluster must license inferences) and homeostasis (mechanisms must hold the cluster together under perturbation). Some of Santana's permissibly-included items pass these tests and some don't; the paper says which.
3. **The slippage diagnosis.** Santana treats the three ontologies as parallel objects of study. He doesn't identify the failure mode where a researcher uses evidence about one cluster to underwrite a claim about another (the "label-sharing produces overclaims" pattern). HPC's projectibility constraint plus the cluster taxonomy makes slippage a diagnosable failure, with worked examples.
4. **The "coordinative label" move.** Santana entertains and dismisses the idea of a single "language-chimera" combining all three ontologies. The paper sharpens this: the highest *language* label is a coordinative label without realist commitment unless it can earn its own projection target and higher-level stabilizers. Coordination across fields is real work; it doesn't entail a unified kind.

The paper should not present Santana's pluralism or Boyd's projectibility-for-a-purpose as its innovation. Both are standing literature. The contribution is the synthesis plus the slippage move.

## The six clusters (consolidation locked 2026-04-26)

Brett's twelve-entry brainstorm consolidates to six firm clusters, plus three open boundary cases.

1. **Capacity.** Human language capacity (subsumes brainstorm #1 and #8). Acquisition, processing, modality flexibility, impairment. Stabilizers: developmental input, neural and cognitive architecture, modality-general abstraction.
2. **Idiolect / I-language.** The linguistic system in an individual's head (Chomsky, Devitt). Subsumes brainstorm #3. Stabilizers: acquisition, entrenchment, individual processing.
3. **Population-level convention / E-language.** Conventional regularities in a community (Lewis, Labov). Subsumes brainstorm #4. Stabilizers: alignment, transmission, social network structure.
4. **Named sociohistorical object.** *English*, *Japanese*, *Cantonese* — institutionally stabilised, identity-marking, named (subsumes brainstorm #2 and #11). Stabilizers: standardization, schooling, literacy, media, legal recognition, language ideology.
5. **Abstract formal system.** Set-theoretic objects, formal grammars, semantic models (Katz, Montague, formal-language theory). Subsumes brainstorm #5. Stabilizers: mathematical inheritance, definitional stipulation.
6. **Communicative practice.** Use, speech acts, interactional uptake (Hymes, Searle, Goffman). Subsumes brainstorm #6 and #7 (with modality as sub-axis). Stabilizers: alignment, metapragmatic typification, joint action.

### Consolidation rationale

- **#1 ≈ #8.** "General human capacity" and "cognitive/neural faculty" are the same thing at different vocabularies (anthropological vs Chomskyan). Merged into Capacity.
- **#2 + parts of #11.** Named sociohistorical objects and identity / standardization / national-language ideology project together. The named-language object IS an identity-marking institutional artefact. Merged into Named sociohistorical object.
- **#6 + #7.** Communicative behaviour and modality (speech/writing/signing) merge as use-plus-instantiation. Modality differences are a sub-axis within Communicative practice; if they start licensing genuinely different inferences during drafting, revisit.
- **#5 vs #12.** Abstract formal systems (Katz Platonism, formal semantics, formal-language theory) and methodological constructs (corpus, grammar-as-analyst's-product) are nominally close but project differently. The formalist's working object is a candidate-Platonist entity; the corpus is a contingent collection of strings. Kept separate; #12 remains open as a candidate seventh cluster.
- **#3 vs #10.** Idiolect / individual grammar and medium of thought are both individual-internal but answer different questions. Kept separate; #10 remains open.

### Settled boundary cases (resolved 2026-04-26)

- **#9 — Broader semiotic system: framing genus, not a cluster.** Treated in §2 as the genus that the six clusters sit within. Animal communication and programming languages remain as boundary cases under Capacity and Communicative practice rather than getting their own section.
- **#10 — Medium of thought: set aside in §2.** A two-sentence note frames LoT as a metaphorical extension — the Fodor 1975 move ported language-like properties (compositionality, productivity) into mental representation as a substantive thesis. The metaphor is real but doesn't share the projectibility profile of the public-object senses. Whether mental representation is language-like is a different debate. Pre-empts the Fodor- / Quilty-Dunn-friendly reader without dragging the paper into mentalese.
- **#12 — Methodological construct: dropped, redistributed.** The label is too broad ("which methods? which fields?"). Corpus-as-data, grammar-as-analyst's-product, competence-vs-performance, treebanks-as-language-proxies span fields with very different commitments. Each gets absorbed into a cluster's stabilizers (corpus crosses #3 and #6 as a data form; competence is #2 idealised; treebanks operationalise #5). The methodological-construct *sense* — claims like "the corpus IS the language" or "competence IS the language" — becomes one of the slippage patterns catalogued in the slippage section. This is the operationalisation-slippage type.

## Per-cluster template

Each surviving cluster gets an entry of the form:

- **Paradigm cases.** What X is uncontroversially in the cluster?
- **Boundary cases.** What X pulls on the cluster?
- **Diagnostics.** How do you tell if X is in the cluster?
- **Stabilizers.** What mechanisms keep the cluster usable for inference? At what grain (micro / meso / macro) do they operate?
- **Projection target.** What does classifying X under this cluster let a field predict, explain, or generalise from? Conditional form: "observed P licenses expected Q in unobserved cases."
- **Defeat conditions.** When does the projection fail?
- **Slippage risks.** Which other clusters does this one get confused with? What overclaims does that confusion produce? The slippage section is the methodological payoff.

## The slippage move (Casten / Pinker as worked example)

Casten et al. 2026 (HAQER / verbal-performance developmental phenotype) is read by Pinker (and others on social media) as evidence about the language faculty. The paper's diagnosis: HAQER evidence is about a developmental capacity that surfaces in verbal performance — closer to cluster 1 (Capacity) but specifically the developmental sub-axis — while "language faculty" claims often imply cluster 1's processing-and-impairment sub-axis or cluster 5's formal-system claims about hierarchical structure-building. The slippage is the move from one sub-axis to another, made invisible by the shared word *language*. This is the paper's signature example.

## Architecture (provisional)

### 1. Introduction: a slippage and a question

Open with the Casten / Pinker case (or another live slippage). Show how the shared word *language* lets a behavioural-developmental finding be read as faculty evidence. State the question: how should we use the word *language* across fields, given that the word is doing work for clusters that don't all license the same inferences?

### 2. Pluralism, and what HPC adds

Five components, in order:

1. **Santana engagement.** Cite Santana 2016 directly. Re-express his pluralism (psychological / social / abstract ontologies, three criteria). State his strictures (no isolationism, no agnosticism).
2. **HPC discipline.** Introduce Boyd: cluster + stabilizers + projection. State what HPC adds — projectibility-and-homeostasis tests for realist standing, plus slippage as a diagnosable failure mode.
3. **The semiotic frame.** Note the broader semiotic genus (animal communication, programming languages, formal symbol systems generally) within which the six human-language clusters sit as species. Mention as framing; do not give it its own section.
4. **Language of thought, set aside.** Two sentences: LoT (Fodor 1975, Carruthers, Quilty-Dunn et al. 2023 — verify before citing) is a metaphorical extension that ported language-like properties into mental representation. The metaphor is real but doesn't share the projectibility profile of the public-object senses. Whether mental representation is language-like is a different debate; the paper doesn't take a position.
5. **Operationalisation-slippage anticipation.** Note that each cluster gets operationalised differently in research practice (corpus, treebank, grammar-as-analyst's-product, competence/performance idealisation), and that claims like "the corpus IS the language" are a slippage pattern the paper will catalogue in §9.

### 3-8. The six clusters

One section per cluster, following the per-cluster template. Order of presentation TBD (provisional: Capacity → Idiolect → Population convention → Named object → Formal system → Communicative practice, building from individual to institutional, ending with use). ~1000-1200 words per section.

### 9. Slippage

The methodological payoff. Catalogue slippage patterns:
- *Capacity → faculty narrowed* (Casten / Pinker: developmental verbal-performance phenotype read as faculty evidence). Worked example.
- *Idiolect → population* (Chomskyan I-language claims defended by E-language data, or vice versa).
- *Named object → convention* (treating a standardised national language as if it were the population's spoken convention).
- *Formal system → empirical object* (projecting Katz-Platonist properties onto observed linguistic behaviour).
- *Operationalisation → ontology* ("the corpus IS the language", "competence IS the language", "the treebank IS the grammar").

Show how naming the slippage clarifies disputes.

### 10. The umbrella label

What is *language simpliciter*? A coordinative label across fields. Real work — coordinating across linguistics, cognitive science, anthropology, philosophy is genuinely useful — but no realist commitment unless it earns its own projection target and higher-level stabilizers.

### 11. Conclusion

Replace "What is language?" with better questions: "Which cluster is in play? What does classifying X under this cluster let the field predict? What slippage risks attend the use of the word here?"
