# Per-cluster template — Capacity

Drafted 2026-04-26 from the source-grounding pass. This file populates the per-cluster template for the Capacity cluster (subsumes brainstorm #1 "general human capacity" and #8 "cognitive/neural faculty"). It is the first cluster template; per HANDOFF, Capacity is templated first because it anchors the §1 hook (Casten / Pinker).

## Headline

The Capacity cluster, as it travels in the literature, is **mechanistically heterogeneous** — fat in the technical sense of HPC-book chapter 8: one label covers mechanistically distinct sub-axes whose stabilizers and projection targets pull in different directions. The discipline the paper supplies is per-sub-axis: identify which sub-axis a Capacity claim is really about, identify the stabilizers maintaining the cluster at that grain, and check whether the evidence in hand actually projects for that target.

The HCF 2002 / PJ 2005 / FHC 2005 / JP 2005 exchange is itself an instance of this discipline being negotiated in real time. HCF tried to distinguish FLB from FLN (a sub-axis distinction). PJ argued that recursion-only is too narrow, that phonology and word-learning belong in the uniquely linguistic faculty. FHC clarified that PJ blurred the FLB/FLN distinction. JP added that FHC's "current utility / functional origin" dichotomy omits current adaptation. The whole exchange is a clarification dance about which sub-axis any given claim is really targeting.

## Sub-axis inventory

The Capacity cluster as it functions in the literature contains at least five sub-axes:

1. **Capacity-as-faculty / processing-architecture (FLN sub-axis).** The abstract computational system: what is uniquely human and uniquely linguistic. Recursion (HCF Hypothesis 3) vs phonology + words + most of syntax (PJ contesting).
2. **Capacity-as-broad-faculty (FLB sub-axis).** FLN + sensory-motor + conceptual-intentional + interfaces. Most components shared with other species (theory of mind, vocal-tract anatomy, conceptual representations, vocal imitation).
3. **Capacity-as-developmental-acquisition.** Species-specific rates and modes of language acquisition; sensitive period; babbling; vocabulary learning. The Casten et al. F1 (sentence repetition) sits here.
4. **Capacity-as-comparative-biology / vocal-learning.** Cross-mammalian convergence on vocal-learning regulatory architecture. Songbirds, dolphins, parrots, cetaceans, pinnipeds. The HAQER cross-mammalian convergence sits here.
5. **Capacity-as-impairment-phenotype.** SLI, FOXP2-related disorders, dyslexia, stuttering. Genetic-architecture inferences (the genetic-evidence section of PJ 2005).

The five are not independent: they overlap, and individual studies often touch more than one. But they license different inferences and have different stabilizers, so claims travelling between them require explicit warrant.

## Per-sub-axis template

### 1. Capacity-as-faculty / processing-architecture (FLN)

- **Paradigm cases.** The narrowly-syntactic recursive computational system; Merge as the binary-tree-building operation (Minimalist Program); X-bar phrase structure; long-distance dependencies; binding; control; the operations that map sound onto meaning at the abstract level. Whatever turns out to be uniquely human and uniquely linguistic in the abstract computational system.
- **Boundary cases.** Phonology (HCF made no mention; PJ argue it has discrete infinity but is not technically recursive — syllables can't embed in syllables). Morphology (PJ argue it's continuous with syntax; HCF's framework leaves it underspecified). Pirahã (per Everett 2004; reportedly lacks recursion in PJ's reading; status disputed). Recursion in visual cognition (JP 2005 argue this shows recursion isn't unique to language). Recursion in number cognition (HCF speculate FLN may have evolved for number / navigation / social relations; PJ counter that recursive number cognition appears parasitic on language).
- **Diagnostics.** Behavioural: AnBn / phrase-structure grammar acquisition (Fitch & Hauser 2004 with cotton-top tamarins). Neural: brain-area dissociations across linguistic vs nonlinguistic recursion. Theoretical: derivability via Merge or equivalent operation. Computational: ability to handle hierarchical phrase-structure dependencies of unbounded depth.
- **Stabilizers.** Contested. Classical Chomskyan view: innate genetic endowment (UG) at the individual level, presumably with a small number of recently-evolved genetic loci. Pinker-Bloom 1990 / PJ 2005 alternative: piecemeal selection for communication shaping multiple components, with FLN being the convergence of several such selected components rather than a single recently-evolved adaptation. Maintenance grain: individual (Level 1 in HPC-book chapter 8 levels-and-bearers) plus genetic-population grain.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a structural feature, e.g., agreement, binding, displacement) licenses expected Q (a typological universal, an acquisition-order prediction, a constraint on language-specific variation) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the feature is acquired by mechanisms shared with non-faculty cognition (PJ's argument about word-learning); the feature varies cross-linguistically in ways UG-style theories can't accommodate; the feature isn't actually unique to language (recursion in visual cognition, music, motor sequencing).
- **Defeat conditions.** A class of putatively faculty-specific properties acquired via demonstrably general cognitive mechanisms. (PJ 2005 cite SLI's deficits across multiple grammatical domains as evidence the FLN-recursion-only story is wrong; Pirahã as evidence recursion may not be universal; FOXP2's domain-spanning effects as evidence against single-mutation faculty-emergence stories.) Asymmetric fraying (P1 from the Predictions appendix): if structural-acquisition predictions hold for some structural domains but fail uniformly for others, the FLN cluster is fraying along sub-domain lines.
- **Slippage risks within the Capacity cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 2: recursion-only debate. HCF assign vocal imitation to FLB; PJ argue the human capacity is specifically a "capacity to learn to produce speech," not a general capacity for vocal imitation, so it's continuous with FLN-style language-specific capacities. **The dispute is in part about which sub-axis vocal-imitation-of-speech belongs to.**
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 5: SLI / FOXP2 evidence read as architecture evidence. PJ make this move legitimately as a defeat-of-recursion-only argument. The slippage occurs when impairment phenotypes are assumed to read off architectural detail directly without addressing how impairment generates the surface deficit.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Capacity-1 ↔ **Abstract formal system.** The recursion-only hypothesis is closely tied to Minimalist-Program theoretical commitments. PJ 2005 argue the recursion-only hypothesis "is motivated by Chomsky's recent approach to syntax, the Minimalist Program, which de-emphasizes the same aspects of language. The approach, however, is sufficiently problematic that it cannot be used to support claims about evolution." When the FLN claim is read as a claim about the formal-system character of language rather than about a cognitive faculty, the slippage is to the Abstract formal system cluster.
  - Capacity-1 ↔ **Idiolect / I-language.** Chomsky uses "I-language" for the mental object an individual speaker has; FLN is the species-uniformly-shared abstract system. The label "language faculty" travels between these. (Santana addresses this directly: criterion-(2) for the psychological ontology can be satisfied by either i-language or FLN, but the inferences they license differ.)

### 2. Capacity-as-broad-faculty (FLB)

- **Paradigm cases.** Sensory-motor system (vocal-tract anatomy, descended larynx, vocal imitation, categorical perception of speech, neurophysiology of action-perception). Conceptual-intentional system (theory of mind, conceptual representations, referential vocal signals, intentional communication). Computational mechanisms for recursion (FLN). The interfaces between these.
- **Boundary cases.** Memory, respiration, digestion, circulation — necessary but not sufficient for language; HCF explicitly exclude. The conceptual-intentional system overlaps substantially with primate cognition (HCF acknowledge; PJ concur with caveats about uniquely human conceptual subsystems like ToM, ownership, multi-part tools).
- **Diagnostics.** Comparative-biology methodology: identify homology vs analogy across species. Anatomical: vocal-tract structure, neural correlates of language processing. Behavioural: theory-of-mind tasks across species; vocal-learning capacities; categorical perception of speech.
- **Stabilizers.** Largely shared with other species: phylogenetically deep neural / developmental architecture. Maintenance grain: comparative-biology / cross-species (the regulatory genomics of Casten et al. operate at this grain too — HAQER-like sequences across 49 vocal-learning mammals).
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a sensory-motor or conceptual capacity) in species X licenses expected Q (the same capacity in species Y, with quantitative or qualitative differences depending on the inheritance pattern) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: P turns out to be an analogous (independent) evolution rather than a homology; P has no behavioural or developmental analogue across the predicted species range.
- **Defeat conditions.** A claimed FLB component that turns out to be uniquely human (which would re-classify it as part of FLN rather than FLB). A claimed FLB component that has no comparative analogue (which would suggest mis-attribution to FLB). Per Hypothesis 1 of HCF: if all of FLB turned out to be strictly homologous to animal communication, the broader strong-uniqueness story would weaken.
- **Slippage risks within the Capacity cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 4: FLB and comparative-biology overlap substantially. The comparative-biology sub-axis is the methodological framework for testing claims about FLB. Slippage occurs when comparative-biology evidence is read as direct evidence about FLN architecture without addressing whether the evidence applies to a shared component or a unique one.
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 1: see Sub-axis 1's slippage risks above (the FLN/FLB boundary is the central point of disagreement in the HCF/PJ exchange).
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Capacity-2 ↔ **Communicative practice.** FLB includes the conceptual-intentional system, which overlaps with what's needed for communication. Claims about "what humans do communicatively" can be read as FLB claims (cognitive architecture) or as Communicative-practice claims (about uses, speech acts, interactional uptake). Hymes / Goffman / Searle work at the latter grain; the slippage is when their data are read as evidence about cognitive architecture.

### 3. Capacity-as-developmental-acquisition

- **Paradigm cases.** First-language acquisition: rate of vocabulary acquisition (massive in human children, vanishingly slow in trained apes per HCF); phonological acquisition (sensitive period; categorical perception tuned to ambient phonology); morphosyntactic acquisition; emergence of grammar across childhood. Sentence repetition (Casten et al. F1) as a developmental aggregate phenotype. Critical-period effects.
- **Boundary cases.** Sign-language acquisition (modality-general but maintains the same developmental trajectory). Heritage-language attrition (Polinsky 2018 — peripheral items drift, core holds; this is partly a Capacity-3 phenomenon but also a Population-convention phenomenon). Late-acquired second languages (don't show the same trajectory). Home-sign systems (Senghas & Coppola 2001 on Nicaraguan Sign Language emergence).
- **Diagnostics.** Behavioural: longitudinal and cross-sectional measures of vocabulary growth, phonological discrimination, grammatical acquisition. Developmental-cohort studies (Casten et al.'s EpiSLI cohort N=350 with 17 longitudinal assessments K-4). Cross-population variation: heritability estimates from twin studies.
- **Stabilizers.** Developmental input (interaction with caregivers; ambient language environment). Neural and cognitive architecture (working memory, attention, lexical retrieval, motor speech, articulation — the elements that load on Casten's F1). Modality-general abstraction (children acquire spoken or signed languages with the same trajectory). Maintenance grain: individual developmental trajectory + population-level statistical patterns. Operates at HPC-book Levels 1 and 2 (individual disposition + population distribution).
- **Projection target.** Observed P (an acquisition pattern in a cohort or individual) licenses expected Q (the same pattern in unobserved children of similar age and input environment, OR a clinical / educational outcome in unobserved older outcomes) in unobserved cases. Casten et al.'s explicit projection: HAQER-CP-PGS β = 0.18 on F1 in EpiSLI is replicated (with phenotypically coarser measurement and accordingly smaller effect) in SPARK, ABCD, UK Biobank. Defeated if D: replication fails systematically; F1 turns out to capture mostly cognitive-domain-general variance rather than language-specific variance.
- **Defeat conditions.** Asymmetric fraying (P1): if the developmental-trajectory cluster fails along expected lines (e.g., later-acquired structures fray under perturbation while early-acquired structures persist), the maintenance-mechanism story is supported; if fraying is uniform or random, the developmental-cluster story is undermined. Conditioning gain (P5): F1 should remain predictive after conditioning on cohort-specific input variables; if it does not, the developmental-acquisition story is conflated with general cognitive variance.
- **Slippage risks within the Capacity cluster.**
  - **Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 1: this is the §1 hook.** Casten et al.'s F1 (sentence repetition, primarily a developmental verbal-performance phenotype) is read in the popular reception (Pinker tweet, 2026-04-25) as evidence for Capacity-as-faculty (the Pinker-Bloom 1990 modular language faculty). The within-cluster slippage is from the developmental-acquisition sub-axis (where the measurement is) to the faculty / processing-architecture sub-axis (where the inference lands). The cohorts (EpiSLI children K-4) are not measuring faculty architecture; they're measuring a developmental phenotype that loads heavily on sentence repetition.
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 5: SLI / impairment evidence is partly developmental, partly architectural. Studies use developmental cohorts to make inferences about architecture. The slippage is at the inference step.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Capacity-3 ↔ **Idiolect / I-language.** What an individual learner ends up with is an idiolect / I-language. Slippage occurs when developmental-acquisition data are treated as direct evidence about the steady-state idiolect, ignoring the maintenance mechanisms operating beyond acquisition.
  - Capacity-3 ↔ **Population-level convention.** Children acquire community-level patterns (per Wiese 2023 on com-sits, Kinzler 2007 on accent over race). The acquisition trajectory itself is partly population-conditioned. Slippage occurs when population-level conventions are read as evidence about individual developmental capacity (or vice versa).

### 4. Capacity-as-comparative-biology / vocal-learning

- **Paradigm cases.** Vocal-learning species (songbirds, parrots, cetaceans, pinnipeds, bats). Cross-mammalian regulatory convergence (Casten et al.'s HAQER-like sequences across 49 vocal-learning mammals + 121 nonvocal-learner species, controlling for phylogenetic relatedness). Comparative neurophysiology of action-perception systems. Vocal-tract anatomy comparisons. The descended larynx in humans and several other mammalian species.
- **Boundary cases.** Apes' poor vocal imitation (HCF note this; PJ accept and add that the "capacity for vocal imitation" in humans is really a "capacity to learn to produce speech"). FOXP2 conservation across vertebrates with human-specific changes. Cetacean and pinniped vocal learning (analogous, not homologous, to human speech-learning capacities). Songbird "subsong" / babbling parallels with human infant babbling. Mirror neurons and gestural-imitation accounts of language origin.
- **Diagnostics.** Cross-species genomic comparison (HAQER-like / HAR-like sequence-similarity scores controlling for phylogeny). Cross-species behavioural assays of vocal learning. Comparative neuroanatomy of speech-related regions. Comparative perceptual-discrimination studies (chinchillas / macaques on phoneme boundaries; Kuhl & Miller 1975 etc.).
- **Stabilizers.** Phylogenetically deep regulatory architectures (transcription-factor binding sites, chromatin accessibility patterns at HAQER-like loci). Convergent selection pressure on vocal-learning across mammals (the "obstetric trade-off" story Casten et al. develop). Selection on auditory-cortex temporal processing. Maintenance grain: cross-species phylogenetic + within-species genetic-population.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a regulatory architecture or behavioural capacity in species X) licenses expected Q (a similar architecture / capacity in species Y, conditional on convergent selection pressure or shared inheritance) in unobserved species. The Casten et al. cross-mammalian projection: HAQER-like sequence similarity correlates with vocal-learning ability when phylogenetic relatedness is controlled. Defeated if D: convergence claims fail to replicate when additional vocal-learner species are sampled; the HAQER-vocal-learning correlation is driven by a confound with body size or some other phylogenetic covariate.
- **Defeat conditions.** A claimed convergent feature that turns out to be a phylogenetic artifact under denser sampling. A claimed shared capacity that doesn't appear in additional members of the convergent group. The cross-species projection breaking down (per Casten et al.'s own caveat that effect sizes in the replication cohorts were "relatively modest" and that "continued investigation of HAQER effects across diverse cognitive domains will be valuable").
- **Slippage risks within the Capacity cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 4 ↔ Sub-axis 2: FLB and comparative biology overlap; see above.
  - Sub-axis 4 ↔ Sub-axis 3: cross-species genomic findings are read as evidence about within-species developmental capacity. The two sub-axes share a substrate (the genome) but operate at different grains. Casten et al.'s methodology — using a developmental cohort (EpiSLI) to validate genomic associations and then projecting cross-species — explicitly traverses both sub-axes; the slippage occurs when readers conflate "HAQER associated with F1 developmental phenotype" with "HAQER associated with vocal-learning capacity across mammals" without addressing that these are different evidential moves.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Capacity-4 ↔ **Capacity-1 (faculty).** The biggest slippage in the §1 hook: cross-species comparative-biology evidence read as evidence about a uniquely human language faculty. Pinker's Pinker-Bloom-1990-friendly reading of Casten et al. does this: the cross-mammalian convergence story is read as supporting modular-faculty claims, but the convergence is precisely about a feature shared across vocal-learning mammals, not unique to humans. **This is the cleanest cross-sub-axis slippage available, and it's exactly what the §1 hook should foreground.**

### 5. Capacity-as-impairment-phenotype

- **Paradigm cases.** Specific Language Impairment (SLI). FOXP2-related disorders (the KE family; Lai et al. 2001, Bishop 2002, Vargha-Khadem et al. 1995). Dyslexia. Developmental stuttering. Aphasias more broadly (though those are more lesion-driven than developmental).
- **Boundary cases.** Williams syndrome (relatively spared language, impaired visuospatial cognition — a dissociation argued to support modularity). Down syndrome (broad cognitive impairment with specific language difficulties). Autism spectrum disorders (per Casten et al. — SPARK is autism-enriched and replicates the HAQER finding with an autism-skewed cohort). The ABCD cohort uses verbal learning, the UK Biobank uses verbal working memory and educational attainment — these are coarser proxies.
- **Diagnostics.** Behavioural: standardized language assessments differentiating impaired from typical performance. Genetic: linkage and association studies (FOXP2, SLI Consortium 2002, more recent GWAS work). Neural: imaging studies of impaired vs typical processing.
- **Stabilizers.** Specific genetic loci with measurable effects (FOXP2 mutations causing the KE-family phenotype). Polygenic background (Casten et al.'s HAQER-CP-PGS architecture, distributed across 1763 SNPs). Developmental cascades from early molecular differences to later behavioural phenotypes. Maintenance grain: individual genetic + neural + developmental.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a specific genetic variant or impairment phenotype) licenses expected Q (a related deficit in unobserved cases sharing the variant; or a related variant in unobserved cases sharing the deficit) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: replication fails; the proposed impairment-phenotype cluster doesn't hold under stricter behavioural assessment.
- **Defeat conditions.** A class of impairments grouped under one label (SLI) that turns out to be mechanistically heterogeneous (per the SLI Consortium's findings of multiple loci). A genetic finding that doesn't replicate (the now-substantial methodological literature on PGS replication and effect-size shrinkage). The PJ 2005 argument: if the recursion-only hypothesis predicted heritable impairments that completely or partially knock out recursion but leave speech perception and production comparable to those of chimpanzees, and the actual impairment literature instead shows disorders that span multiple components of language (FOXP2, SLI), then recursion-only is undermined.
- **Slippage risks within the Capacity cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 5 ↔ Sub-axis 1: see Sub-axis 1's slippage risks above (impairment evidence read as architecture evidence).
  - Sub-axis 5 ↔ Sub-axis 3: impairment-phenotype findings come from developmental cohorts; the inference moves from "this child has these scores" to "this genetic variant causes this developmental trajectory." The slippage is when the developmental-trajectory story is read as a fixed genetic-architecture claim.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Capacity-5 ↔ **Idiolect / I-language.** Impairment phenotypes manifest at the level of individual idiolects, but the inference often runs to claims about the species-typical faculty. The Devitt-style sociolinguistic answer would reframe SLI as variation in the mechanisms maintaining a particular idiolect, not as evidence about UG.

Most-frequently-traversed slippage edges:
- 3 → 1 (the §1 hook). Casten et al. measure F1 (developmental); Pinker reads as faculty.
- 4 → 1. Cross-mammalian convergence read as faculty evidence.
- 5 → 1. Impairment evidence read as architecture evidence.
- 1 → 2. FLN claims drifting into FLB territory (or vice versa) — the central HCF/PJ disagreement.

## The Casten / Pinker worked example for §1

The §1 hook (cleanest framing):

1. Casten et al. (2026) measure F1 in EpiSLI (N = 350 children, K-4, 17 longitudinal assessments; F1 primarily driven by sentence repetition). HAQER CP-PGS shows β = 0.18 on F1 (P = 1.2 × 10⁻⁴, FDR-adjusted P = 0.004), with no association on nonverbal IQ (β = 0.06, P = 0.19, FDR-adjusted P = 0.61). HAQERs comprise less than 0.1% of the genome but carry 188× more predictive power per SNP. Cross-species comparison: HAQER-like sequences correlate with vocal-learning capacity across 49 vocal-learning mammals + 121 non-vocal-learners (170 species total). Replication in SPARK (P = 7.9 × 10⁻⁵), ABCD (P = 0.048), UK Biobank (P = 0.002), with phenotypically coarser measures.

2. The findings are about Capacity sub-axis 3 (developmental verbal-performance phenotype) anchored by HAQER architecture from sub-axis 4 (cross-mammalian regulatory convergence on vocal-learning).

3. Pinker (Twitter, 2026-04-25) cites the paper as vindication of Pinker & Bloom (1990). Pinker-Bloom 1990 is a specific argument about Capacity sub-axis 1 (the modular language faculty as a complex adaptation shaped by natural selection for communication). The reading converts F1 + HAQER evidence into faculty-architecture evidence.

4. The slippage is sub-axes 3 + 4 → sub-axis 1. The cohorts measure developmental verbal performance, not faculty architecture. The cross-species data show convergence across vocal-learning mammals, not human-specific faculty. The sub-axis 1 inference Pinker draws requires additional evidence that isn't in the Casten et al. paper — specifically, evidence that the F1 phenotype tracks faculty architecture rather than general developmental verbal performance.

5. Diagnosing the slippage in HPC terms:
   - Cluster check: each sub-axis has its own property cluster (developmental phenotype properties; cross-species genomic properties; faculty-architecture properties). They overlap in extension (HAQERs participate in all three substrates) but the projection targets differ.
   - Homeostasis check: the developmental phenotype is maintained by individual + population-level developmental machinery; the cross-species architecture is maintained by phylogenetically deep regulatory selection; the faculty (if real) would be maintained by UG-style species-uniform genetic endowment. **Different stabilizers, different defeat conditions.**
   - Projectibility check: F1 evidence projects to clinical and developmental prediction, not to faculty-architecture claims. Cross-species genomic evidence projects to comparative neurogenetics, not to human-specific faculty claims.

6. The diagnosis is not that Pinker is wrong about Capacity sub-axis 1 in general — it's that this evidence isn't direct evidence for that sub-axis. Pinker-Bloom 1990's adaptive-design argument might still be right; Casten et al. just doesn't supply the missing evidence.

## Length and integration notes for §3 drafting

§3 (the Capacity cluster section in the eleven-section architecture) should:

- Open with the sub-axis structure (the five sub-axes, briefly named).
- Devote roughly equal prose to each sub-axis: paradigm cases, stabilizers (named), projection target (in conditional form), defeat conditions, slippage risks (within-cluster + cross-cluster).
- Anchor each sub-axis with one or two source-grounded examples (HCF / PJ exchange for sub-axis 1; vocal-tract / vocal-imitation for sub-axis 2; Casten / EpiSLI for sub-axis 3; HAQER cross-mammalian for sub-axis 4; FOXP2 + SLI for sub-axis 5).
- Close with the cross-sub-axis slippage map and a forward reference to §9 where the slippage diagnostics are catalogued more systematically.

Word budget estimate: 1500-2000 words for §3, with the sub-axis structure carrying most of it. The cluster is fat enough to warrant the longest §3-§8 section; later clusters can be tighter because their projection targets are less internally contested.

## What this template doesn't yet address

- The Hauser et al. 2014 "mystery of language evolution" paper (in Mendeley but not source-grounded here) — may add useful material on what the field has learned and not learned about Capacity over the decade since HCF.
- Christiansen & Chater 2015 "the language faculty that wasn't" — a usage-based account of natural language recursion, available in Mendeley. Worth source-grounding before §3 final draft if PJ-style critique is to be balanced with a usage-based rebuttal.
- Specific acquisition-literature citations beyond the survey level — read primary literature for any specific claim about acquisition rates, phonology / morphology / syntax acquisition timelines, sensitive-period phenomena.
- The Pirahã / Everett 2004 controversy as a boundary case for Sub-axis 1. PJ 2005 cite Everett 2004 (an unpublished MS at the time) without further engagement; the subsequent published literature is contested.
- The Jackendoff Parallel Architecture as PJ's positive alternative to HCF. The PJ 2005 paper frequently cites Jackendoff 2002 (Foundations of Language); the alternative may need explicit characterization to ground the "language is an adaptation for communication, evolved piecemeal" thesis.

These are flagged as read-on-demand; they don't block §3 drafting.
