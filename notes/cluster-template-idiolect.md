# Per-cluster template — Idiolect / I-language

Drafted 2026-04-26 from the source-grounding pass. This file populates the per-cluster template for the Idiolect / I-language cluster (subsumes brainstorm #3 — the linguistic system associated with an individual). It groups Chomsky's I-language and Devitt's linguistic-conception view at the individual scale.

## Headline

The Idiolect / I-language cluster is fat in a particularly contested way. Unlike Capacity (where sub-axes differ in scale and methodology), this cluster contains a deep **ontological dispute** about what the cluster's primary entity actually is: a mental object (Chomsky's psychological conception) or a realm of physical SLEs the individual is competent over (Devitt's linguistic conception). The dispute is not resolved by methodological refinement — it's a disagreement about what `language at the individual level` *is*. The HPC discipline applied here is to specify which sub-axis a claim is about, identify the stabilizers maintaining the cluster at that grain, and check whether the evidence in hand actually projects for that target.

Santana 2016 places both Chomsky and Devitt in the psychological ontology (since both make individual-internal claims), but Devitt's actual position is that linguistics is *not* psychology (Devitt 2003, 2008). Devitt's "linguistic conception" makes the grammar's subject matter a non-psychological realm of physical expressions whose linguistic properties are conventionally established (citing Lewis 1969 explicitly). This is closer to a hybrid of Santana's psychological and social ontologies — and pushes against the natural fit with either.

## Sub-axis inventory

The cluster contains at least three sub-axes:

1. **I-language (Chomskyan psychological conception).** The mental object — the speaker's language faculty, the system of rules and representations in the mind. Chomsky 1986 *Knowledge of Language*, 1995 *The Minimalist Program*, 2013 Dewey lectures.
2. **Idiolect (Devittian linguistic conception).** The individual's command over a non-psychological realm of physical SLEs (sounds, inscriptions, gestures) with conventionally-established linguistic properties. Devitt 2003, 2006 *Ignorance of Language*, 2008.
3. **Empirical individual variation.** The fact of differential competence within heterogeneous speech communities (Hymes 1992, Bloomfield's White Thunder example, Dabrowska 2012 on individual variation). The empirical-psycholinguistic study of what different individuals know and produce, relatively neutral on the deeper ontological dispute.

These project to different inferences. Sub-axis 1 projects to claims about innate cognitive architecture; sub-axis 2 projects to claims about external linguistic reality; sub-axis 3 projects to claims about distribution of competences across populations. Slippage occurs when claims travel between them under the shared "individual-level grammatical knowledge" label.

## Per-sub-axis template

### 1. I-language (Chomskyan psychological conception)

- **Paradigm cases.** The system of rules in a competent speaker's mind that generates the well-formed sentences of their language. Chomsky's notion of "the steady state" reached after acquisition. Universal Grammar (UG) as the species-general genetic endowment that shapes which I-languages are humanly possible. The minimalist program's Merge as a candidate-universal computational primitive.
- **Boundary cases.** Multilingual speakers (multiple I-languages, or one I-language with multiple parameter settings?). Code-switching (does a code-switch represent crossing between two I-languages, or one I-language with a productive bilingual subsystem?). Heritage speakers with reduced I-language commands. Speakers with delayed-acquisition or impaired I-language. LLMs (Chomsky-aligned commentators have been emphatic that LLMs lack I-language; the question is whether the lack matters for any other cluster's claims).
- **Diagnostics.** Acceptability judgement experiments treated as windows onto I-language. Construction of grammars with explanatory adequacy at the level of UG. Acquisition data treated as evidence about the initial state and the maturational path. Aphasia and SLI data as evidence about the cognitive architecture's modularity.
- **Stabilizers.** Internal: the genetic endowment fixing UG; the maturational mechanisms shaping initial state to steady state; entrenchment / consolidation in adulthood. External: linguistic input from the community (the trigger data). Maintenance grain: HPC-book Levels 1 (individual disposition) and the genetic-population grain. Note: the I-language conception has trouble accommodating the social-conditioning grain that HPC book chapter 16 highlights (per Santana's discussion of Chomsky's anti-social-ontology arguments).
- **Projection target.** Observed P (an acceptability judgement; an acquisition pattern; an SLI/aphasia phenotype) licenses expected Q (claims about UG; claims about the steady state; claims about the language module's architecture) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the same P pattern can be derived from non-mental mechanisms (e.g., distributional statistics learnable from input); the predicted Q fails to replicate cross-linguistically; the I-language posits don't constrain the predicted patterns more tightly than alternative frameworks.
- **Defeat conditions.** Hymes's heterogeneous-community evidence against the homogeneous-speech-community assumption. Dabrowska 2012 individual-variation evidence: judgements correlate with exposure history rather than uniform UG-shaped competence. Devitt 2008's argument: even if the structure rules of the language are real and known, that doesn't imply they're "in the mind" beyond the minimal Respect Constraint. PJ 2005's evidence against recursion-only as the unique I-language contribution: phonology, words, agreement, case all show language-specific properties, suggesting I-language (if it exists) is more than UG-recursion-only.
- **Slippage risks within the Idiolect cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 2: this is the central Devitt-vs-Chomsky disagreement. I-language claims are smuggled into linguistic-conception arguments by treating "the speaker's grammar" as both the mental system and the structure rules of the public language. Devitt's three distinctions are designed exactly to keep these separate.
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 3: I-language claims drift between "what's species-uniformly shared" (UG-level) and "what an individual happens to have acquired" (steady-state / idiolect-level). Different evidence licenses different sub-axis claims.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Capacity (cross-cluster).** Hauser-Chomsky-Fitch 2002 explicitly distinguish FLN from FLB and from individual I-language; the slippage occurs when I-language claims (steady-state) are read as Capacity claims (faculty-architecture) without that distinction.
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Population-level convention.** Chomsky's E-language is not (in Santana's reading) a real entity; for Chomsky, "English" is just a convenient label for a set of similar I-languages. The slippage runs the other way: when E-language-style claims about the community are read as evidence about I-language. (Santana rebuts Chomsky's position; sub-axis 1 in our cluster is the locus of the relevant Chomskyan view.)

### 2. Idiolect (Devittian linguistic conception)

- **Paradigm cases.** Individual sentences and expressions a speaker produces and understands. The speaker's command over the conventionally-established linguistic properties (syntactic, semantic, phonological) of physical sounds, inscriptions, and gestures. Devitt's "Standard Linguistic Entities" (SLEs): sounds and inscriptions with linguistic properties established by convention.
- **Boundary cases.** Devitt distinguishes from the I-language sub-axis sharply: his claim is that the grammar describes the linguistic reality, not the speaker's mental state (beyond the minimal "(M)": there's something-we-know-not-what in the speaker that respects the structure rules). Boundary cases include: SLEs that are theoretically problematic (Rey's "intentional inexistents"; Devitt 2008 contests Rey at length). Phonological abstractions (phonemes — Rey claims they're inexistents; Devitt argues they're conventional but real). Computer-generated SLEs (LLMs producing physical inscriptions with linguistic properties — does the LLM count as competent over them? Devitt's framework says no, because no convention-based "mutual understanding" with intended meaning).
- **Diagnostics.** Analysis of expressions and their structural relations. Acceptability judgements treated as evidence about the linguistic reality (not the mental state). Tests of expressions' linguistic properties (e.g., constituency tests, binding tests) interpreted as discovering structure in the linguistic reality, not in the mind.
- **Stabilizers.** Convention (Lewis 1969 as the canonical theory of how convention is established). Mutual understanding within speech communities. Individual entrenchment / habit. Maintenance grain: distributed across Levels 1, 2, 3 (HPC book ch 8) — the conventional properties are population-level, but individual command of them is at Level 1.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (the linguistic properties of expressions of one type) licenses expected Q (linguistic properties of unobserved expressions of similar type, given convention extension and structural-similarity reasoning) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the conventional properties don't actually hold for the predicted cases; the speaker's command over the relevant SLEs is more limited than assumed; novel linguistic entities don't take on conventional linguistic properties as expected.
- **Defeat conditions.** Rey's argument that linguistic entities are intentional inexistents (Devitt 2008 §5 contests this at length but the question remains active). The argument that conventions cannot be established for non-overt / abstract syntactic positions like PRO (Collins's challenge, Devitt 2008 §4). The argument from speaker-level variability: if individuals differ enough about which sounds count as which expressions, the SLEs claim weakens (Rey's worry; Devitt's counter is that variation just means many conventions, not no conventions).
- **Slippage risks within the Idiolect cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 1: see Sub-axis 1's discussion.
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 3: Devitt's sub-axis is theoretically motivated (the linguistic conception is the right ontology for grammars). Sub-axis 3 is empirically motivated (the actual fact of individual variation). Slippage when the empirical sub-3 evidence is taken to settle the sub-2 ontological question (or vice versa).
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Population-level convention.** Devitt's view depends on convention (Lewis 1969) for the linguistic properties of SLEs. The convention is at the population level (sub-axis 2 of the Population-convention cluster). The slippage is treating Devitt's claims as being only about individuals when they presuppose population-level conventions. Devitt acknowledges this but locates the *explanatory* interest at the individual-expression level.
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Abstract formal system.** Devitt explicitly rejects assimilation to Katz Platonism. SLEs are physical; their linguistic properties are conventional, not abstract-Platonist. But the slippage is real: the structural properties of expressions look formal, and someone could read Devitt's emphasis on grammar-as-theory-of-expressions as a Platonist-leaning move (which Devitt rejects).

### 3. Empirical individual variation

- **Paradigm cases.** Hymes 1992's White Thunder vs Red Cloud Woman (Bloomfield 1927) — both Menominee speakers but with very different linguistic resources. Multilingual speakers' command of multiple varieties (Hymes's NE Amazon example with four-language scope). Heritage speakers' competence (Polinsky 2018; HPC book ch 16). Dabrowska 2012 on individual variation in grammaticality judgements correlating with exposure history. Differential competence within a single speech community (sociolinguistic variables tracked across speakers).
- **Boundary cases.** Aphasic / SLI speakers (boundary with Capacity sub-axis 5). Heritage speakers (boundary with Capacity sub-axis 3). Speakers commanding multiple varieties / registers (boundary with Communicative practice sub-axis 3).
- **Diagnostics.** Sociolinguistic-interview data on individual variation. Acceptability-judgement experiments with attention to inter-speaker variance. Corpus data conditioned on speaker. Heritage-language attrition studies. Multilingual production / comprehension experiments.
- **Stabilizers.** Acquisition history (variable across speakers). Entrenchment based on individual usage frequency (Sassenhagen et al. 2018 on low-frequency verbs). Network density at the individual level. Maintenance grain: Level 1 (individual disposition) primarily; Levels 2-3 to the extent that individual variation tracks population-level conditioning.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (an individual speaker's variation pattern) licenses expected Q (similar variation in similar individuals; predictable variation under conditioning on input history) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: variation is uniform random (no recoverable structure); variation is fully predicted by population-level convention without residual individual signal.
- **Defeat conditions.** Per HPC book Predictions appendix D2: structure-without-lift — if individual variation in the cluster proves to be a random walk, the maintenance claim for individual-level differences collapses. Per P5: conditioning on individual-level variables (input history; network density) should improve prediction; if it doesn't, the cluster fails the conditioning-gain test.
- **Slippage risks within the Idiolect cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 1: empirical individual-variation data are sometimes read as evidence about the I-language steady state (the variation reveals different I-languages); other times as evidence against the I-language framing (the variation is too unconstrained for UG-shaped competences).
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 2: empirical individual-variation data are evidence about which conventional SLEs an individual is competent over. Slippage when sub-3 data are taken to bear on sub-2's deeper ontological question.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Population-level convention.** Individual variation is also population-level distribution of variation. The two are linked — population-level convention is what individual idiolects deviate around. Slippage when individual-variation findings are read as direct evidence about population-level conventions, or vice versa.
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Communicative practice.** Differential competence in Hymes's sense includes appropriateness as well as grammatical competence; sub-axis 3 of Idiolect can shade into Hymes's communicative competence (Communicative practice sub-axis 3).

## Cross-sub-axis slippage map (within the Idiolect / I-language cluster)

```
  1. I-language (Chomsky, psychological conception)
                  \   /
                   \ /
                  ←→
                   |
  2. Idiolect (Devitt, linguistic conception)
                  \   /
                   \ /
                  ←→
                   |
  3. Empirical individual variation (Hymes, Dabrowska, sociolinguistics)
```

Most-frequently-traversed slippage edges:
- 1 ↔ 2 (psychological vs linguistic conception — the Devitt-Chomsky-Collins exchange is the canonical example)
- 1 ↔ 3 (I-language steady-state vs empirical individual-variation findings)
- 2 ↔ 3 (Devitt's framework presupposes Lewis-style convention; the empirical-variation data are the test bed for whether such conventions hold)

| Idiolect sub-axis → other cluster | What gets smuggled |
|----------------------------------|--------------------|
| Sub-axis 1 → Capacity | I-language steady-state claims read as evidence about Capacity-faculty-architecture |

## Worked example: the Devitt–Chomsky-Collins-Rey exchange as cluster-distinction work

Devitt 2008 is itself a worked example of within-cluster sub-axis slippage being negotiated. The participants are:

- Chomsky (and the Chomskyan tradition): I-language is the explanatory target; grammars are theories of the mental language faculty.
- Devitt: the linguistic conception — grammars are about non-psychological linguistic reality (SLEs); the psychological reality is what's left over (the minimal Respect Constraint).
- Collins (Chomskyan critic of Devitt): denies there's any non-psychological linguistic reality worth studying.
- Rey (antirealist): denies SLEs exist at all (intentional / perceptual inexistents).

Each participant takes the same primary data (acceptability judgements, structural patterns, acquisition findings) and reads it as evidence for a different sub-axis of the Idiolect cluster (or an adjacent cluster). The disagreement is not resolved by adding more data — it's about what the data are evidence of.

This is exactly the slippage-as-diagnosable-failure-mode pattern the paper foregrounds. The HPC discipline:
- **Cluster check.** Does property covariance hold? Yes — the data exist and recur.
- **Homeostasis check.** Are there identifiable mechanisms? *Different mechanisms for different sub-axes.* Sub-axis 1: cognitive architecture + UG. Sub-axis 2: convention + mutual understanding + entrenchment. Sub-axis 3: input variation + entrenchment + network density.
- **Projectibility check.** Does the cluster project? *Different projection targets.* Sub-axis 1 projects to UG-shaped predictions about cross-linguistic stability. Sub-axis 2 projects to conventional-extension predictions about novel SLEs. Sub-axis 3 projects to individual-level variation under input perturbation.

The exchange itself is a clarification dance: each side accuses the other of confusing sub-axes. The HPC framing makes the disagreement diagnosable: each side is making real claims about a different sub-axis of the cluster, and the disagreement is exactly about which sub-axis the cluster's primary entity belongs to.
