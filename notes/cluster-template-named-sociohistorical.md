# Per-cluster template — Named sociohistorical object

Drafted 2026-04-26 from the source-grounding pass. This file populates the per-cluster template for the Named sociohistorical object cluster (subsumes brainstorm #2 — institutionally-stabilized named varieties — and parts of brainstorm #11 on identity / standardization). It is the cluster of `language` claims about *English, Japanese, Cantonese, AAVE, Quebec French, Modern Standard Arabic*: institutionally stabilised, identity-marking, named.

## Headline

The Named sociohistorical object cluster is the cluster of `language` claims about *named varieties stabilised by institutions over historical time*. Unlike Population-level convention (which projects to community-level coordination patterns) or Communicative practice (which projects to situated communicative work), this cluster projects to claims about an institutionally-named, historically-tracked entity: *English*, *Tagalog*, *Cantonese*, *AAVE*, *Modern Standard Arabic*. The cluster is centrally about **how a community of conventional regularities gets named, bounded, and politically realised** as a thing — and what that institutional realisation does to the regularities themselves.

The cluster is at the centre of two methodological disputes. First: **does the named object exist?** Pennycook 2010 / Makoni & Pennycook frame named languages as inventions that "emerge from local practice" rather than primary entities. Chomsky (Santana 2016 §4) argues that named-language objects ("English") are "not real-world objects but artificial, somewhat arbitrary, and perhaps not very interesting constructs." Both critiques converge on a sceptical conclusion. The HPC framing rebuts: named objects are real *as institutionally-stabilised attractor basins* — but they are sustained by additional stabilizers (standardization, schooling, literacy, media, legal recognition, language ideology) that distinguish them from sub-axis 2 of the Population-level convention cluster. Second: **what work does the named object do?** Looping-kind effects (Hacking 1999) operate strongly here — once a name exists, speakers orient to it, schools teach it, courts recognise it, prestige is allocated through it, and the named object's properties feed back into the conventional regularities it supposedly merely labels.

## Sub-axis inventory

The cluster has at least three sub-axes:

1. **The named object as institutional artefact.** *English* / *French* / *Cantonese* as a thing schools teach, courts use, governments standardize, dictionaries codify, language tests assess, and citizens claim or disclaim. The institutional infrastructure that gives the named object its scale and durability.
2. **The named object as identity-marker / index of belonging.** Speakers orient to named varieties as belonging-criteria (Quebec French / Anglo-Quebec; AAVE / Mainstream English; Modern Standard Arabic / vernacular Arabic). Enregistered voices (Agha 2005), iconisation and erasure (Irvine & Gal 2000), language ideology (Silverstein, Bourdieu).
3. **The named object as descended kind.** Phylogenetic / sociohistorical lineage: descent from earlier states (Old English → Middle → Modern), interaction with substrates and superstrates, contact-induced changes, creolisation. Genealogical continuity as a stabilizer in its own right.

These project to different inferences. Sub-axis 1 projects to predictions about the institutional weight a named object carries (which forms get marked correct in school, which appear in legal records). Sub-axis 2 projects to indexical inferences about identity claims (who counts as a Cantonese speaker; what claiming AAVE indexes). Sub-axis 3 projects to historical-linguistic reconstructions and contact-language predictions. They share stabilizers — institutional infrastructure, ideological reinforcement, transmission across generations — but ask different questions.

## Per-sub-axis template

### 1. Named object as institutional artefact

- **Paradigm cases.** *English* as the language of British, American, Australian, and other education systems; the language of legal records in many Anglophone jurisdictions; the language of the IETF; the language of academic publishing in many fields. *Modern Standard Arabic* as a deliberately-codified pan-regional standard. *Bahasa Indonesia* as a colonially-engineered national-language project. *Israeli Hebrew* as a revival project. The standardization moves Haugen and others have catalogued (selection, codification, elaboration, acceptance). Dictionaries (OED, Merriam-Webster, Académie française) as institutional codifiers. Language tests (TOEFL, IELTS, JLPT) as gatekeepers. Linguistic-rights legislation (Quebec's Bill 101; Welsh language acts).
- **Boundary cases.** Languages without strong institutional infrastructure (most heritage languages; many indigenous languages with limited literacy / standardization). Emerging named varieties (Singlish; Spanglish if institutionally recognised). Variants that resist institutional capture (AAVE — recognised academically but not institutionally privileged in most US settings). Pluricentric languages (Spanish across Spain, Mexico, Argentina with separate codification traditions). Disputed boundaries (Croatian / Serbian / Bosnian; Hindi / Urdu; Mandarin / Cantonese — particularly contentious). The "internet English" non-cluster (HPC book ch 16: too internally diverse to be a single cluster — a fat class).
- **Diagnostics.** Presence of standardizing institutions (national academies, codifying dictionaries, school curricula). Presence of standardised orthography. Legal recognition (official-language status, language-rights frameworks). Presence of large-scale literacy infrastructure. Population-level survey data on which named variety speakers claim. Census data on language identification.
- **Stabilizers.** Schooling and literacy education (the most pervasive). Standardization institutions (academies, dictionaries, textbooks). Mass media and entertainment (which forms get broadcast / printed at scale). Legal and administrative recognition (courts, government services, ID documents). Language tests and certification regimes. Diaspora maintenance institutions (Sunday schools, community centres). Publishing standards. **Material reinforcement** in HPC-book ch 8 sense: curricula, exams, style guides, tagsets, parsers — institutions that materially reinforce the named cluster. Maintenance grain: HPC-book Levels 3 and 4 primarily — conditioning variables and institutional loops.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a feature is institutionally codified as part of named variety V) licenses expected Q (the feature appears in V's edited registers reliably; speakers oriented to V's institutional gatekeepers will avoid V's stigmatized variants; the feature will be transmitted through V's literacy infrastructure to the next cohort) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the institutional reinforcement weakens and the feature fails to persist (asymmetric fraying P1); the feature's distribution doesn't track institutional channels in the predicted way; the named variety turns out to be a fat class covering institutionally-distinct sub-objects (e.g., "internet English").
- **Defeat conditions.** A claimed named object whose institutional infrastructure is too weak to maintain the cluster (passes the cluster check but fails homeostasis). A named object that doesn't pass the projectibility check across its institutional channels (fails for some channels but not others — fat-class signature). The Pennycook 2010 / Makoni & Pennycook critique: the named object is an invention, and the institutional infrastructure produces what it claims to merely codify. HPC framing of this critique: not a defeat condition for the cluster but a methodological caveat — the cluster's reality is *as an institutionally-stabilised attractor basin*, not as a pre-existing thing institutions merely label.
- **Slippage risks within the Named sociohistorical object cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 2: institutional codification and identity-marking are tightly coupled — institutions are how identity-marking gets scale. Slippage occurs when one is treated as the whole story.
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 3: institutional history vs sociohistorical descent are conceptually distinct. The institutionally-named "English" and the descended-from-Old-English lineage are not exactly the same cluster (the institutional name covers a fat class of varieties with different descent paths; AAVE has a different sociohistorical descent than Mainstream English, but both are institutionally-named "English"). Slippage when descent claims are read as institutional claims, or vice versa.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Population-level convention.** Population conventions are sub-named-language: AAVE conventions, RP conventions, Glaswegian conventions are all conventions within the named "English" object. The slippage is treating named-language claims as direct evidence about specific population-conventions, or vice versa. The HPC book ch 16 example: "internet English" as a fat class because it covers Reddit, formal blogs, arxiv preprints — too much internal diversity. The named object straddles populations and registers.
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Idiolect/I-language.** Devitt 2008 worries about variability of idiolect — Rey points out that what counts as the same "English" varies too widely for the SLEs claim to be other than a sociological one. The HPC reframing: yes, the named object is a sociological / institutional construct; that doesn't dissolve it as a real cluster, but it does locate the cluster's grain at the institutional level rather than at any individual speaker's level.

### 2. Named object as identity-marker / index of belonging

- **Paradigm cases.** Quebec French as central to Quebecois identity. AAVE as central to Black American linguistic identity. Modern Standard Arabic vs vernacular Arabic in the Arab world. Welsh as a revival-project identity-marker. Hebrew in modern Israel. Romanesh / Romanish in Switzerland. The waiter's "and yourself?" example (HPC book ch 16) — the indexical work attaches to a regional / sub-cultural sense of place. Eckert 2008 indexical fields, when ideology hardens, become emblematic of named varieties (per Agha 2005).
- **Boundary cases.** Diaspora and code-mixing varieties (heritage Spanish in the US; British Punjabi). Emerging post-colonial Englishes (Indian English, Singapore English). Cases where claiming a named variety is itself contested (who counts as a "real" speaker of X). Iconisation and erasure (Irvine & Gal 2000) — qualities a named variety is taken to embody, often masking the heterogeneity within.
- **Diagnostics.** Survey data on language attitudes and self-identification. Studies of indexical fields (Eckert 2008) for named-variety-indexing variables. Discourse analysis of metapragmatic talk *about* named varieties. Ethnographic studies of language-and-identity practices. Looping-kind dynamics — Hacking 1999 — once a named variety is institutionally recognised, speakers' awareness of the recognition shapes their identity-claims and use.
- **Stabilizers.** Convergence + divergence dynamics from HPC book ch 16: convergence within the named-variety community tightens the cluster; divergence from outside sharpens boundaries (in-group vs out-group accent maintenance). Identity-marking pressures. Reflexive social cognition (the metapragmatic level). Looping-kind feedback (Hacking 1999). Ideological work (Silverstein 2003 indexical orders, especially the n+1st re-analysis under ideological pressure). Agha 2005 enregisterment dynamics. Maintenance grain: Levels 2, 3, 4 — population, conditioning, institutional. Identity-marking is intrinsically multi-level.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (the indexical association of a named variety with an identity) licenses expected Q (speakers claiming the identity will orient to the named variety; community members will recognise the indexical association in unobserved cases; ideology / institutional shifts will reshape the indexical field around the named variety) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: indexical claims about the named variety fail to replicate cross-community; the variety isn't actually doing identity-work for the population claimed; ideological-shift predictions don't track named-variety dynamics.
- **Defeat conditions.** A named variety with no measurable identity-indexical work (would suggest it's only Sub-axis 1 institutional, not Sub-axis 2 identity). A named variety whose identity-association doesn't survive ideological shifts (suggesting fragility). The cross-cultural transfer test: does the named-variety identity-marker function in similar ways in similar cultural-political contexts? If not, the claim is fragile.
- **Slippage risks within the Named sociohistorical object cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 1: identity-marking and institutional infrastructure are tightly coupled (see above).
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 3: identity-claims often invoke descent ("our grandmothers spoke like this"). Slippage when descent claims are taken to settle identity-claims (or vice versa). Iconisation / erasure (Irvine & Gal) is exactly the move where a complex sociohistorical descent gets simplified into an identity-emblem — which is a slippage detectable in HPC terms by asking which stabilizers actually maintain the supposed link.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Communicative practice (sub-axis 4 metapragmatic).** Indexical fields and enregistered voices (Comm-practice cluster sub-axis 4) overlap heavily with identity-marking on named varieties. The two clusters share machinery; the difference is grain. Communicative practice asks about situated indexical work; Named sociohistorical object asks about how the practice gets bundled into a named institutionally-stabilised object.
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Population-level convention.** Identity-marking dynamics typically operate on Population-convention sub-axis 3 (stylistic / indexical practice) variables and then get bundled into named-object identity-claims. Slippage when identity-claims about the named object are read as evidence about population-convention variables, or vice versa.

### 3. Named object as descended kind (sociohistorical lineage)

- **Paradigm cases.** Old English → Middle English → Modern English: a continuous lineage with traceable phonological, morphological, syntactic changes. The Romance languages descended from Latin. The Slavic languages from Common Slavic. The various creoles' lineages from contact between specific superstrates and substrates. Tok Pisin's emergence from Melanesian Pidgin English. Pennycook (Pennycook 2010) and Makoni argue that even these lineages are constructions — the named lineages are imposed on continuous variation.
- **Boundary cases.** Mixed languages and creolisation events (where the lineage is not single-source). Language-shift scenarios (where the named object's continuity is sociologically rather than linguistically straightforward). Pluricentric varieties with separate codification (American vs British English share descent but diverged institutionally). Reconstruction of Proto-languages (the named-object claim is at a depth where direct evidence is unavailable). Names that get retroactively applied to earlier states ("Old English" is a 19th-century construct applied to texts the writers wouldn't have called by that name).
- **Diagnostics.** Historical-linguistic methodology (regular sound correspondences; lexical comparison; reconstruction). Phylogenetic-linguistic methods (computational phylogenetics applied to language families). Sociohistorical research on language-shift and contact events. Manuscript and textual evidence.
- **Stabilizers.** Iterated transmission across generations. Standardization decisions that anchor a "snapshot" of the named object (literary canons, dictionaries, school texts). Educational continuity — students learn the named-object as historical-continuous-with-the-current-state. Institutional reinforcement of the lineage narrative (the OED, etymological resources, historical-grammar handbooks). Maintenance grain: distributed across all four levels of HPC-book ch 8, but with particular weight at Level 4 (institutional loops — the lineage narrative is institutionally maintained).
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a feature in a historical state of the named object) licenses expected Q (predictable diachronic changes in the lineage; predictable correspondences with related languages; predictable reconstructions of unattested earlier states) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the predicted historical changes don't match the textual record; reconstructions fail to predict the divergent descendants; the lineage claim turns out to be sociologically constructed rather than continuous.
- **Defeat conditions.** A named-object lineage that turns out to be substantially constructed by post-hoc institutional narrative (Pennycook / Makoni-style critique of "English" descended from "Old English" as a unitary thing). Asymmetric fraying along sociohistorical lines (some sub-varieties showing very different lineage traces). The contact-language case where the lineage is hybrid rather than single-descent.
- **Slippage risks within the Named sociohistorical object cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axes 1, 2: descent claims are often invoked to legitimate institutional and identity claims. The slippage is treating the linguistic lineage as warrant for the institutional / identity claim that the lineage is supposedly evidence for.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Capacity (cross-cluster).** Historical-linguistic claims about descent intersect with claims about capacity-as-acquisition (children acquiring named varieties) and capacity-as-comparative-biology (named-language families and their phylogenetic relations). Slippage when descent-claims are read as evidence about capacity-architecture.
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Population-level convention.** Descent operates through populations of speakers; the lineage is the historical record of conventional changes. Slippage when descent-claims are read as direct evidence about contemporary population-convention variables.

## Worked example: the Pennycook / Makoni "disinventing languages" critique

The strongest extant critique of this cluster is the Pennycook 2010 / Makoni & Pennycook framework: named languages are *inventions*, not pre-existing entities that institutions merely label. Pennycook (2010) argues that language is "a local practice" — language emerges from situated activity in specific places and times. Named languages are post-hoc bundlings of those local practices into named, countable, system-like things, and the bundling is largely a colonial / institutional project.

The HPC reframing:

- **The critique is partly right.** Named-language objects are institutionally-stabilised abstractions, not pre-existing natural kinds. Pennycook is correct that "English" is a name that gets imposed on a set of practices, not a pre-existing thing the practices instantiate.
- **The critique is partly overstated.** Once an institutional / ideological apparatus exists that names, codifies, teaches, and recognises a variety, the named object becomes real *as an institutionally-stabilised attractor basin*. Speakers orient to it. Schools transmit it. Courts use it. Looping-kind dynamics (Hacking 1999) make the named object an active force in shaping the practices it supposedly merely names. To deny the named object's reality is to ignore the institutional infrastructure that maintains it.
- **The HPC discipline:** the named object is a real cluster *for institutional and identity-related purposes*; it is *not* a cluster for individual-cognitive purposes (where Idiolect/I-language is the right grain) or for moment-by-moment communicative purposes (where Communicative practice is the right grain) or for community-level convention purposes (where Population-level convention is the right grain). The cluster's reality is grain-relative, and the grain it works at is institutional. Pennycook's critique correctly identifies that the named object isn't real at *some* grains; the HPC framing adds that it *is* real at the grain its stabilizers operate.

This dispute is itself a **cross-cluster slippage example for §9**: critiques of the named-object cluster's reality often slip between grains, treating the institutional-grain claim as if it were a cognitive-grain or convention-grain claim.
