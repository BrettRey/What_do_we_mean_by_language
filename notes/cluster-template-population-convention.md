# Per-cluster template — Population-level convention / E-language

Drafted 2026-04-26 from the source-grounding pass. This file populates the per-cluster template for the Population-level convention cluster (subsumes brainstorm #4 — conventional regularities at the community level). It groups Lewis-style coordination conventions, Labov-style speech-community variables, and Eckert-style stylistic indexical fields.

## Headline

The Population-level convention cluster is the cluster of `language` claims about *what regularities a community of speakers maintains together*. Unlike Idiolect/I-language (about individual speakers) or Named sociohistorical object (about institutionally-stabilised named varieties), this cluster is at the population grain *between* the individual and the named object: a community of mutually-aligned speakers whose convergent practices give the cluster its identity. It is what Chomsky labels (and dismisses) as E-language; it is what Lewis 1969 theorises as convention; it is what Labov 1972 establishes as the speech community; it is what Eckert 2008 elaborates as the indexical field within which variables carry stylistic meaning.

The cluster is moderately fat. The Lewis-style theoretical framework (population-level convention as coordination equilibrium) projects to different inferences than the Labov-style empirical framework (speech communities maintaining variables in stratified patterns) and the Eckert-style stylistic framework (indexical fields embedding ideology in linguistic form). They overlap on stabilizers — alignment, transmission, communicative pressure — but project to different research questions.

## Sub-axis inventory

1. **Coordination convention (Lewis 1969).** Regularities in behaviour sustained by mutual expectation in coordination problems. Conceptual / philosophical framework.
2. **Speech-community variables (Labov 1972, the variationist tradition).** Empirically-attested orderly heterogeneity at the population level: variables stratified by class, gender, age, ethnicity, region. Methodological backbone: sociolinguistic interviewing + multivariate regression on token frequency.
3. **Stylistic / indexical practice (Eckert 2008, the third-wave variationist tradition).** Variables carry indexical meanings within fluid indexical fields; speakers deploy them to construct personae and stake stylistic claims; meanings are reproduced and reshaped in situated use.

These project to different inferences. Sub-axis 1 projects to stable coordination outcomes given common interest. Sub-axis 2 projects to stratified frequency patterns under demographic conditioning. Sub-axis 3 projects to the social meaning a variable carries when activated in context. They all sit at the population grain but ask different questions.

## Per-sub-axis template

### 1. Coordination convention (Lewis 1969)

- **Paradigm cases.** A regularity R in the behaviour of members of a population P, conditional on a recurrent situation S, such that (a) almost everyone conforms to R; (b) almost everyone expects almost everyone else to conform; (c) almost everyone prefers any one to conform conditional on the others conforming; (d) there is at least one alternative R' that meets the same conditions; (e) all of the above is common knowledge in P. Linguistic conventions: speakers conform to a regularity (use word W to mean M; use form F in context C) because they expect others to and prefer to conform conditional on others conforming. The English/French/Japanese label for "dog" — chien, dog, 犬 — are equivalent solutions; what makes one rather than another the convention in a community is just that everyone expects everyone to use that one.
- **Boundary cases.** Cases where common knowledge is partial or contested (emerging conventions; declining conventions). Cases where a regularity exists but isn't sustained by mutual expectation (innate behaviours; physical regularities). Cases where alternatives don't exist or aren't salient (one-solution coordination problems). Cases where the population is too thinly connected for common knowledge (large-scale "language" communities where most members never interact). Lewis-style convention straining at the edges of large named-language objects (Sub-axis 4 cluster boundary).
- **Diagnostics.** Game-theoretic analysis of coordination structure. Behavioural test: does the regularity persist under perturbation of individual deviation? Common-knowledge probes: do members report awareness of others' expectations? Lewis's own characterisation: a "platitude" that "language is ruled by convention" (Lewis 1969: 1).
- **Stabilizers.** Mutual expectation among community members. Communicative success as the payoff function (speakers prefer to conform because it makes communication work). Iterated coordination producing common knowledge. Maintenance grain: HPC-book Levels 2 and 3 — population-level distribution + conditioning variables (since conventions are typically conditioned on context).
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a regularity in behaviour at the population level) licenses expected Q (the regularity persists under perturbation; alternative coordinations are equally stable in principle but not actual; the regularity tracks common-interest structure of the situation) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the regularity persists despite individual deviation (suggesting it's not actually convention-sustained); the regularity has no salient alternatives (suggesting it's a non-coordinative regularity); the population isn't connected enough for common knowledge to be plausible (suggesting Lewis's framework doesn't apply at this grain).
- **Defeat conditions.** Davidson's "no such thing as a language" puzzle (linguistic innovation undermines stability; Santana 2016 §4 reframes this as a productive scientific question rather than a defeat). The argument that named-language conventions are too thinly connected for Lewis-style common knowledge to hold across whole national-language communities (handled in Sub-axis 4 cluster). The empirical question of whether speakers actually have common knowledge of conventions or just behaviourally converge — the conditioning-gain test (P5): does conditioning on common-knowledge variables (mutual exposure; feedback from interaction) improve prediction?
- **Slippage risks within the Population-level convention cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 2: Lewis-style theoretical convention vs Labov-style empirically-measured variables. Slippage occurs when a Labov-empirical pattern is read as Lewis-theoretical convention without showing that mutual-expectation conditions hold — or, conversely, when Lewis's theoretical apparatus is taken to settle questions that Labov's empirical work answers differently.
  - Sub-axis 1 ↔ Sub-axis 3: Lewis treats convention as relatively static once established; Eckert's indexical field is fluid and constantly reshaped by stylistic practice. Slippage when one is mistaken for the other.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Idiolect / I-language.** Devitt 2008 explicitly grounds his linguistic conception in Lewis-style convention. The slippage is treating individual idiolect data as direct evidence about Lewis-conventions, ignoring the population-level grain.
  - Sub-axis 1 → **Named sociohistorical object.** Lewis's framework can in principle apply at named-language scale, but the population is so vast and loosely connected that common knowledge is strained. The slippage is treating named-language regularities (English, French, Japanese) as straightforwardly Lewis-conventional when they're sustained by additional institutional infrastructure (Sub-axis 4).

### 2. Speech-community variables (Labov 1972, Tagliamonte, etc.)

- **Paradigm cases.** Labov's New York City department-store study (post-vocalic /r/ stratified by SES). Martha's Vineyard centralized /ay/ (Labov 1963) — a variable acquiring local-authenticity meaning within the fishing community. The Northern Cities Vowel Shift (and other regional sound changes). Tagliamonte's variationist work on York English (zero article, demonstratives, future tense). Variables stratified by gender (women lead in changes from above and below — Labov 2001). Stable variables vs changes in progress.
- **Boundary cases.** Variables at the boundary between dialect and register (e.g., g-dropping / (ING) — variable across speakers but also across contexts within speakers). Variables in transition (just becoming markers from indicators, in Labov's terms). Heritage-language attrition (Polinsky 2018) — speakers showing reduced control of community variables. Cross-cultural cases where the speech-community concept is harder to apply (multilingual urban contexts).
- **Diagnostics.** Sociolinguistic interview methodology (Labov's standard). Multivariate regression analysis on token-frequency data. Apparent-time vs real-time analysis. Random forests / Bayesian variants (Tagliamonte & Baayen 2012). Quantitative indices of network density / network cohesion (Milroy & Milroy).
- **Stabilizers.** Interactive alignment within the speech community (Pickering & Garrod 2004). Identity-marking (speakers maintain variables that index group membership). Network density: dense local networks maintain non-prestige variants; loose networks accept change-from-outside (Milroy 1987). Iterated transmission across generations. Maintenance grain: HPC-book Levels 1, 2, 3, 4 — distributed across all four; the speech-community concept is one of the most multiply-stabilised in linguistics.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a variable's distribution in a sociolinguistic-interview corpus) licenses expected Q (the same distribution in unobserved similar speakers; predictable change trajectories under demographic shift; predictable convergence/divergence under network perturbation) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the distribution doesn't replicate when the speech community is sampled differently; the variable isn't stably maintained under perturbation; conditioning on demographic variables doesn't recover the expected pattern (the conditioning-gain test).
- **Defeat conditions.** Asymmetric fraying (P1): if the cluster's stabilizers weaken, peripheral variables should erode before core ones; if erosion is uniform / random, the cluster is fragile. Conditioning gain (P5): conditioning on speaker-internal variables (input history, network density) should improve prediction over a pooled-population baseline; if it doesn't, the speech-community claim is unsupported. The mixed-bin problem from HPC book ch 16 — pooled data conflates conditioned systems.
- **Slippage risks within the Population-level convention cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 1: see Sub-axis 1 above.
  - Sub-axis 2 ↔ Sub-axis 3: Labov's variables are correlational; Eckert's indexical-field framework asks what the variables mean in stylistic practice. Slippage when Labov-correlations are read as evidence about social meaning, or when Eckert-style stylistic-meaning analyses are read as evidence about coarse demographic correlations.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Idiolect / I-language.** Speech-community variables are realised in individual idiolects. Slippage when population-level patterns are read as direct evidence about individual cognitive structures, or vice versa.
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Named sociohistorical object.** Speech communities are nested inside named-language objects (the AAVE speech community within "English"; the Pittsburgh speech community within "American English"). Slippage when speech-community claims are read as named-language claims (or vice versa) without addressing the institutional-stabilizer differences.
  - Sub-axis 2 → **Communicative practice.** Labov-style variation studies often overlap with Hymes-style differential-competence work. The two are conceptually distinct: variation studies pattern-match across speakers; differential-competence studies study what individuals know.

### 3. Stylistic / indexical practice (Eckert 2008)

- **Paradigm cases.** Eckert's (ING) example: the velar variant indexes education, intelligence, articulateness — and by extension pretension, condescension. The apical variant indexes casualness, lack of effort — and by extension laziness or unpretentiousness. Hearers interpret a token's indexical value based on the style (and content) in which it's embedded. Martha's Vineyard /ay/ centralization (Labov 1963 reread by Eckert) — the fishermen aren't just claiming to be Vineyarders but making a claim about what a Vineyarder is. Persona style: the styles associated with types in the social landscape (Valley girls, cholos, jocks, burnouts). The waiter's "and yourself?" (HPC book ch 16) — enregistered as deferential service speech. Pittsburgh / NYC / Detroit urban personae.
- **Boundary cases.** Cross-community variation in indexical meaning (the same form indexes different things in different communities — Johnstone & Kiesling 2008, cited by Eckert). Forms whose indexical meaning is contested or shifting. Forms acquiring new indexical values through stylistic appropriation (the Vineyard fishermen's reanalysis of /ay/ centralization). Internet-mediated stylistic practice (memes, micro-registers, platform-specific styles).
- **Diagnostics.** Matched-guise experiments (e.g., Campbell-Kibler 2007 on (ING)). Ethnographic studies of stylistic practice. Discourse analysis of metapragmatic talk (Silverstein 2003 on indexical orders 1, 2, n+1). Studies of indexical-field-evolution over time.
- **Stabilizers.** Reflexive social cognition (the metapragmatic level: speakers know what their forms index). Convergence + divergence dynamics (HPC book ch 16: convergence tightens clusters from within; divergence sharpens boundaries from without). Continual reproduction of indexical meaning in situated use. Looping feedback (Hacking 1999). Erasure (Irvine & Gal 2000) — ideological foregrounding of some properties of a community while erasing others. Maintenance grain: Levels 2, 3, 4 — population, conditioning, institutional. Indexical-field dynamics are intrinsically multi-level.
- **Projection target.** Observed P (a variable's indexical field, mapped from speech / matched-guise data) licenses expected Q (the variable's social meaning when deployed in unobserved similar contexts; predictable persona-style associations; predictable shifts in indexical value when ideological context changes) in unobserved cases. Defeated if D: the indexical field doesn't replicate cross-community; ideological-shift predictions fail; the variable's meaning is stable rather than fluid (suggesting Eckert's framework over-elaborates).
- **Defeat conditions.** A variable that turns out to have a single fixed meaning across communities (would suggest Eckert's indexical-field framework over-elaborates a simpler reality). A variable whose ideological associations don't change despite ideological shifts in the wider community (would suggest the field is more inert than the framework predicts). Indexical predictions failing when conditioning on ideologically-relevant context-variables (P5 test for indexical claims).
- **Slippage risks within the Population-level convention cluster.**
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 1: Lewis's static convention vs Eckert's fluid indexical field. The frameworks aren't direct competitors — Lewis is at the level of basic coordination on form-meaning; Eckert is at the level of stylistic / ideological indexicality on top of that. But slippage occurs when one is treated as the whole story.
  - Sub-axis 3 ↔ Sub-axis 2: Eckert's third-wave framework explicitly engages with Labov's first-wave variationist methodology and reframes it. Slippage when Labov-correlational findings are read as Eckert-stylistic findings without doing the qualitative ideological / ethnographic work.
- **Slippage risks to other clusters.**
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Communicative practice.** Eckert's indexical field overlaps substantially with the Communicative-practice metapragmatic-typification sub-axis (Sub-axis 4 of that cluster). The two clusters differ in grain: Communicative-practice asks about situated communicative work; Population-convention asks about the population-level distribution of indexical resources.
  - Sub-axis 3 → **Named sociohistorical object.** Indexical fields can become hardened into enregistered voices (Agha 2005), and enregistered voices become emblematic of named varieties. Slippage when indexical-field dynamics at the population level are read as evidence about named-language objects (which are sustained by additional institutional infrastructure).

## The mixed-bin problem (from HPC book ch 16) as a worked example

The single most important methodological commitment for this cluster is **"no conditioning gain, no maintenance claim"** (HPC book ch 16). The mixed-bin problem:

- Pooled data over a population conflates conditioned systems (different dialects, registers, discourse communities). The pooled distribution looks noisy.
- Conditioning on the relevant variables (who is speaking; in what context; to whom) recovers tight coupling.
- A claim that a regularity is real and population-maintained should imply that conditioning on the relevant maintenance variables improves prediction. If conditioning produces no gain, the maintenance claim is unsupported.

Worked example: existential *there's* / *there are*. Pooled English data shows variability — *there's two men* alongside *there are two men*. Looks like agreement is variable, even noisy. Conditioning on register (casual speech vs edited prose) recovers two tight systems: in casual speech, *there's* acts as an invariant presentative particle (Krejci & Hilton 2022, cited HPC book ch 16); in edited prose, agreement is categorical. **One pooled curve isn't one grammar.**

This commitment runs through all three sub-axes of the Population-level convention cluster. It is also the cluster's main cross-cluster slippage diagnostic. Apparent looseness at the population level is often (per the framework) evidence of multi-level conditioning, not evidence against the cluster's reality.
