# Draft Email To James

Subject: Grammaticality as an HPC kind, one small precision

Hi James,

I've been working on the broader "What do we mean by language?" paper, and it exposed a useful pressure point for the grammaticality paper.

The current draft sometimes says that grammaticality is a "single HPC kind." I think the argument still works, but that wording may overstate the unity claim. The better version might be that grammaticality is a recurrent HPC regime, or kind-schema, instantiated locally by conditioned communities.

So not:

> one global extension of grammatical forms across languages and communities

but rather:

> local licensing regimes stabilize morphosyntactic form--meaning pairings through acquisition, repair, entrenchment, social policing, and transmission, and those regimes share enough architecture to support projectible grammaticality judgments.

That would let us keep the response to the pluralist objection, while avoiding the impression that grammaticality is one global kind in the way `language simpliciter` is not. It also fits our own talk about community-relative grammaticality facts, acceptability versus licensing, and the role of register/context.

Possible replacement for the pluralist-objection paragraph:

> While grammaticality is internally complex, the phenomena show enough recurrent unity to constitute a single kind-schema rather than a mere family of homonyms. Local grammaticality regimes differ in extension, but they stabilize the same broad profile: morphosyntactic form--meaning pairings, compatibility constraints, community entrenchment, acquisition, repair, and transmission. Morphological, syntactic, and semantic factors can dissociate, but they are coupled strongly enough within conditioned communities to support projectible licensing judgments.

The broader language paper also gives us a nice way to position grammaticality: as a lower-level HPC case that crosses population convention, idiolect/capacity, communicative practice, and abstract formal systems. That helps explain why acceptability ratings, corpus frequencies, formal grammars, and standard-language norms are all useful evidence, but none is identical with grammaticality.

Brett
