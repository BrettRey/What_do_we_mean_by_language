# Grammaticality And The Language-Cluster Paper

Created 2026-04-26 after asking how the grammaticality theory bears on this paper.

## Core point

The grammaticality paper currently does sometimes say that grammaticality is a single HPC kind. The clearest passages are:

- `Grammar_and_emergence/papers/reynolds-miller-grammaticality.tex`, around the pluralist objection: "the phenomena show sufficient unity to constitute a single HPC kind."
- The conclusion: "grammaticality is a natural kind, realized as a homeostatic property-cluster (HPC) kind."

That wording is defensible only if "single kind" means a recurrent regime type or kind-schema, not one global extension across all languages, communities, registers, and contexts.

The better formulation is:

> Grammaticality is a recurrent HPC licensing regime: conditioned communities stabilize morphosyntactic form--meaning pairings through acquisition, repair, entrenchment, social policing, and transmission. Local grammaticality systems differ in extension, but instantiate the same broad HPC architecture when they support the same kinds of projection.

## Why this matters here

This `What do we mean by language?` paper argues that `language` is not one realist kind but a coordinative label spanning multiple projectible clusters. That puts pressure on any claim that grammaticality is one global kind "of language." The grammaticality paper should not accidentally rebuild `language simpliciter` through the back door.

Grammaticality is best located as a lower-level HPC case that crosses several clusters:

- Population-level convention: community licensing and entrenchment.
- Idiolect/capacity: individual command, acquisition, processing load, acceptability feelings.
- Communicative practice: appropriateness, stance, uptake, register, context-sensitive licensing.
- Abstract formal systems: generated strings and model-theoretic formalisms that diagnose, but do not by themselves settle, community licensing.
- Named sociohistorical objects: standard-language authority and institutional policing, which can influence grammaticality judgments without being identical to grammatical licensing.

## Bidirectional implications

For the language-cluster paper:

- Use grammaticality as a compact worked family of slippages in Section 9.
- Good cases: acceptability-to-grammaticality, processing-to-grammaticality, corpus-frequency-to-grammaticality, formal-grammar-to-community-licensing, and standard-language-to-population-convention.
- The payoff is that grammaticality shows why the same evidence cannot simply travel across capacity, convention, formal system, and communicative-practice clusters.

For the grammaticality paper:

- Replace or qualify "single HPC kind" with "recurrent HPC regime" or "kind-schema instantiated by conditioned communities."
- Make the bearer explicit: not `English` or `language`, but a conditioned population/register/discourse community licensing a form--meaning pairing for a use-domain.
- Keep the unity claim, but make it architectural rather than extensional. Morphological, syntactic, and semantic factors interact strongly enough within local licensing regimes to support projectible judgments, but there is no single cross-community extension of grammatical forms.

## Possible replacement wording

> While grammaticality is internally complex, the phenomena show enough recurrent unity to constitute a single kind-schema rather than a mere family of homonyms. Local grammaticality regimes differ in extension, but they stabilize the same broad profile: morphosyntactic form--meaning pairings, compatibility constraints, community entrenchment, acquisition, repair, and transmission. Morphological, syntactic, and semantic factors can dissociate, but they are coupled strongly enough within conditioned communities to support projectible licensing judgments.

This preserves the grammaticality paper's main thesis while making it compatible with the pluralist map of `language`.
