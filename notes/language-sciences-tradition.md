## Big find: *Language Sciences* vol 61 (2017) is a Love festschrift

Title of the volume: *Orders of Language: A festschrift for Nigel Love*. The volume's central theme is Love's two-orders framework: **first-order language** (embodied, situated, indexical activity) vs **second-order language** (the abstracted, reified objects -- *a language*, *a word*, *a grammatical category* -- generated by reflexive metalinguistic activity). ## Love 2017 (full text now in literature/)

The paper is a response-to-symposium piece anchoring the *Orders of Language* festschrift. The volume grew from the symposium *Orders of Language and the Epistemology of the Language Sciences* held February 2016 at the Centre for Human Interactivity, University of Southern Denmark (organized by Sune Steffensen and Talbot Taylor).

### Love's central thesis

> "Languaging, as an activity, has temporal and logical priority over any language or languages that might be held to be thereby languaged. There must be languaging before there can be languages, both for humanity as a whole and in the life of the individual human being." (p. 117)

> "I propose that we think of a language as a systematised corpus or codification of linguistic abstracta, derived from languaging by decontextualising recurrent [pæʔnz] of phono-semantic similarity and treating those similarities as samenesses." (p. 117)

That is, **a named language is a reified abstraction generated by metalinguistic activity** — by treating phonetically distinct utterances as instances of the same word, repeated across speakers and across time, until a stable codification emerges.

### Love's three orders (not two!)

Section 10 (the "Orders of language" defence) clarifies that the framework has three orders, not two:

1. **First-order** = languaging activity itself (speaking, hearing, writing, reading, signing).
2. **Second-order** = codifications (named languages, words-as-types, languages-as-such) -- abstractions out of recurrent same-treated languaging.
3. **Third-order** = analytical artefacts of metalinguistic analysis (syntactic trees, formal-semantic representations, the linguist's metalanguage). "Entities arising out of the analysis of second-order abstractions. ... Two removes from the first-order utterance, and will almost never play any role in explaining either its production, its interpretation, or whatever unconscious linguistic knowledge might be brought to bear..." (p. 138)

Other contributors to vol 61 push back: Toolan, Mackay, Perregaard, van den Herik, and Cowley (sophistically) all argue first-order languaging is never devoid of second-order constructs. Love's response is the third-order addition plus a clarification that the orders are "a distinction intended to throw light on the scientism at the root of orthodox linguistic thought" (Orman 2016, quoted p. 138), not a hard ontological separation.

### Direct anti-Pinker move (footnote 14)

> "Contra Pinker (1995 [1994] p. 15), who says: 'Simply by making noises with our mouths, we can reliably cause precise new combinations of ideas to arise in each other's minds'."

## What Cowley 2017 (Love's perspective) says (from abstract, fetched)

> "From Nigel Love's perspective, language is a multi-scalar process that connects people, history and nature. In leaving behind the Saussurian tradition's object based views, he contrasts first and second order language. However, the 'orders' do not explain each other and, implicated as they are in change, cannot be defined. Indeed, Love suggests that language can describe everything under the sun -- except language. ... While integrationists celebrate his tautologies as paradoxes, in dialogism, there is a tendency to relate them to theses. ... the Distributed Language Approach traces language to interactivity between ecological beings and, echoing hylomorphism, others trace human plasticity to the interplay of language, nature and peculiar kind of social agency."

## What Cowley 2011 introduction says (from academia.edu OA fetch)

Three opening sections of *Distributed Language* introduction (pp 1--14):

1. **Beyond symbol processing.** Cognition is cultural and embodied; computational/symbolic accounts miss the bodies-in-interaction layer. "Living human beings rely on, not just symbols, but also interactions that sensitise us to each other and our cultural practices."
2. **The distributed perspective.** "Language can be traced to how living bodies co-ordinate with the world." Language functions "by linking people with each other, external resources and cultural traditions."
3. **The Distributed Language Movement: Prehistory.** "Language can be traced to multi-scalar dynamics that spread across groups, artifacts and time-scales."

## How Love's three orders map onto Brett's six clusters

The mapping is partial -- Brett's taxonomy splits Love's orders along stabilizer lines:

| Love's order | Brett's cluster(s) | Comment |
|--------------|-------------------|---------|
| 1st (languaging activity) | Capacity (#1), Communicative practice (#6) | Love treats both as activity; Brett separates by stabilizer (cognitive-developmental vs interactional) |
| 1st--2nd boundary | Idiolect / I-language (#3) | Love is sceptical of mental grammars; Brett treats idiolects as a real cluster with their own stabilizer (individual entrenchment) |
| 2nd (codifications) | Population convention (#4), Named sociohistorical object (#5) | Love's biggest collapse: he doesn't distinguish coordinative regularities from institutionally ratified named objects; Brett does, because they have different stabilizers (interactional alignment vs institutional ratification) |
| 3rd (analytical artefacts) | Abstract formal system (#7) | Direct match |

The HPC contribution to Love's framework: **finer-grained stabilizer-by-stabilizer carving within his orders**, especially second-order. The first-order/second-order separability question Love wrestles with becomes a tractable inter-cluster traversal question with explicit bridge principles.

## Otheguy, García & Reid 2015 (now in literature/, full text)

Direct hit on Brett's named-object-vs-idiolect distinction. Their argument, via the "culinary fable" (a named cuisine isn't defined by ingredients; a named language isn't defined by lexical/structural features):

- **Named languages** ("Spanish", "Catalan", "Mandarin", "Swahili") = social/political constructs. "Defined by the social, political or ethnic affiliation of [their] speakers." "Cannot be defined linguistically." Not, strictly speaking, linguistic objects.
- **Idiolects** = the speaker's personal mental grammar, structured lists of lexical and grammatical features. The proper object of grammatical analysis. Not equivalent to Chomsky's I-language: their idiolect is "acquired primarily through, and deployed mostly in, social and personal interaction," not generated by an instinct.
- **Translanguaging** = "the deployment of a speaker's full linguistic repertoire without regard for watchful adherence to the socially and politically defined boundaries of named (and usually national and state) languages."
- The grammarian "cannot adjudicate" one-language-or-two disputes (Madrid vs Havana vs Lisbon vs Río), because these are political/cultural questions, not lexical/structural ones. Saussure's footnote: "neither languages nor dialects have natural boundaries."

### How Otheguy maps onto Brett's six-cluster taxonomy

Otheguy's framework is **binary**: linguistic (idiolect) vs social (named language). Brett's is **six-cluster**:

- Otheguy's idiolect ↔ Brett's **Idiolect / I-language** cluster (#3)
- Otheguy's named language ↔ Brett's **Named sociohistorical object** cluster (#5)
- BUT Otheguy collapses into "social" what Brett keeps distinct: a population-convention claim (Lewis coordination, Labov variation, Eckert indexical field) is its own cluster (#4) with its own stabilizers; a communicative-practice claim (speech act, register, enregisterment) is yet another (#6); an abstract-formal-system claim is another (#7).

The HPC contribution is therefore not the named-vs-real distinction (Otheguy already does that, well) but the **finer-grained stabilizer-by-stabilizer cluster structure** within what Otheguy lumps as "social." Brett's six-cluster taxonomy is to Otheguy's two-way distinction what Brett's six-cluster taxonomy is to Love's two orders: a more granular continuation of the same diagnostic move.

## Recommended scope (for Brett's decision)

The find changes the calculus. The journal has a curated articulation of the Love programme in vol 61. Brett's HPC pluralism can position itself as a compatible-but-more-granular continuation of that programme: where Love distinguishes two orders, HPC pluralism distinguishes six clusters, three categories proper plus three neighbourhoods, with diagnostics for inter-cluster traversal. That's not a paragraph. That's a **subsection** -- probably ~700--900 words -- placed naturally in §10 (the umbrella discussion) or as a new §10.5.

Concrete pitch:

> "The argument here can be read as a continuation of the *Language Sciences* community's interrogation of the language-as-object myth (Harris; Love 2017; Cowley 2011, 2017; the *Orders of Language* festschrift, vol 61). Where Love distinguishes first-order from second-order language, HPC pluralism distinguishes six clusters with stabilizer-specific projection targets. Three of these (population convention, named sociohistorical object, abstract formal system) are second-order in Love's sense -- objects sustained by reflexive practice and institutional ratification -- but are not interchangeable: a named-sociohistorical-object claim is not a population-convention claim, and confusing them is the Cantonese case from §9.2. ..."

This positions the paper as native to the journal without making Love's framework load-bearing in a way that risks over-commitment.
